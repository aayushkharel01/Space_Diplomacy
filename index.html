Space Saves Lives

Making Satellite Data Mandatory for Disaster Response

My Story: Nepal 2015

I was on the third floor of a house in Nepal, playing with a friend, when the world suddenly began to shake violently. The 2015 Gorkha Earthquake had struck with devastating force.

My mother quickly guided us down and outside to safety. But standing there, watching the dust rise from collapsed buildings across the neighborhood, an overwhelming fear gripped us. My father was teaching at a school far from home.

"In the aftermath, all communication was cut off. We couldn't call him, and he couldn't call us. For several agonizing hours, we felt trapped in uncertainty, unsure if he and our other relatives were safe."

This feeling of helplessness, caused by the simple failure of communication and data flow, has stayed with me ever since. It's why I'm advocating for mandatory Space-Based Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) in every country.

The Golden Period

72 Hours

The first 72 hours after a disaster‚Äîwhen most lives can still be saved. If rescuers can get reliable information about blocked roads, landslides, and collapsed buildings within the first few hours, they can reach thousands more people who are trapped or injured.

The Problem: Traditional ground-based communication systems are destroyed in the very events they're needed for most.

The Solution: Space-Based SOPs

The technology to solve this problem already exists‚Äîin space. Satellites can see through the destruction, map the damage in real-time, and guide rescuers to where they are needed most.

However, most governments fail to leverage this capability effectively because they don't have formal, official protocols for using satellite data instantly during emergencies.

Our Mission: Every nation must adopt mandatory Space-Based Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs). The use of satellite data in national emergencies must become an automatic requirement written into law, not an optional resource that officials debate while people are suffering.
