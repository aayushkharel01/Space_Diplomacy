International Cooperation as a Safety Net

The International Charter

"Space and Major Disasters"

Established in 2000, this multilateral agreement currently includes 17 major space agencies from around the world. The Charter represents one of the most successful examples of international space cooperation for humanitarian purposes.

How It Works

The Charter guarantees free, 24/7 access to satellite data during emergencies for any member country or authorized user. When activated, all participating agencies task their satellites to image the disaster zone and share data through a centralized coordination mechanism.

From hurricanes in the Caribbean to earthquakes in Asia, the Charter provides a critical safety net for disaster response worldwide.

UN-SPIDER

United Nations Platform for Space-based Information for Disaster Management and Emergency Response

While the International Charter provides data access, UN-SPIDER focuses on capacity building‚Äîthe crucial step of ensuring that developing countries can actually use the satellite data effectively.

The Mission

UN-SPIDER works to ensure that all countries, particularly developing nations, have access to and can benefit from space-based information to support the full disaster management cycle‚Äîfrom prevention and preparedness to response and recovery.

Why Capacity Building Matters:

Having access to satellite data is not enough. Countries need trained personnel who can interpret complex imagery, process raw data, and integrate satellite information into existing emergency response plans. Without this institutional knowledge, satellite data remains merely raw information rather than a practical life-saving tool.

The Nepal Example

During and after the 2015 Gorkha Earthquake, UN-SPIDER played a critical role in helping Nepali officials understand and act on the satellite imagery provided through the International Charter. This assistance was invaluable, but it also highlighted the need for countries to develop their own domestic capacity rather than relying on external expertise during crises.

The Path Forward

These diplomatic frameworks provide a strong foundation, but they also highlight the ultimate goal: each nation should develop sufficient domestic capacity‚Äîthrough mandatory SOPs, trained personnel, and processing infrastructure‚Äîto reduce dependency and achieve truly sovereign disaster resilience.

The International Charter and UN-SPIDER prove that global solidarity is possible. Now we must build on this foundation by ensuring every country has the institutional capacity to use these tools effectively and independently.
